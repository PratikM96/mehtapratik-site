How I Run Creative Teams Without Losing Speed or Quality

Agency & in-houseCreative leadership · process6 min read

Managing a creative team is a constant negotiation between speed and quality. Move too fast and the work suffers. Move too slow and the business loses momentum. After leading teams of 15+ creatives across agency and in-house environments, here's the approach I've landed on.

Clarity upfront is speed downstream

The number one cause of slow creative work isn't the creative process, it's unclear briefs. When a designer doesn't understand what they're trying to accomplish, they iterate blindly. When a copywriter doesn't know the audience, they write generically. When a photographer doesn't know what the asset is for, they capture the wrong things.

I spend more time on briefs than most creative directors think is necessary. A good brief defines the goal, the audience, the channel, the deliverable, and the definition of success. It answers the questions a creative would ask before they have to ask them.

Give people ownership, not just tasks

Creatives do better work when they understand how their piece fits the bigger picture. I share context liberally: why we're making this, what the business is trying to accomplish, what success looks like. Then I get out of the way. Micromanaging creative work kills the work. Your job as a director is to set the direction and the standard, then trust the people you hired.

A five-minute conversation at day one saves hours of rework at day five.

Build feedback into the process, not onto the end of it

The worst creative review happens when a director sees the work for the first time at the final stage and requests significant changes. By then the creative has invested hours in a direction that isn't right. That's demoralizing and expensive. I do quick check-ins at the concept stage, before anyone has gone too deep.

Protect the team from chaos, but not from reality

Creative teams need some buffer from last-minute stakeholder changes and shifting priorities. But they also need enough business context to make good judgment calls when things change, because they always do. The best teams I've built can adapt quickly without losing the thread of what they're making and why.
