How We Grew the Social Program, and What the Data Actually Said

SPORTIMEGrowth · content systems · analytics7 min read

When I joined SPORTIME as Brand Lead, the social presence was stagnant relative to the size of the network. By the end of the year, combined cross-platform impressions had climbed from roughly 4.5M to over 11.7M, with nearly 17,000 net new followers added across Instagram and Facebook. Here's what actually drove that, and what I learned reading the data honestly.

Volume matters more than most people admit

The engagement-rate purists will tell you that posting more dilutes your quality metrics. They're right, technically. But there's a bigger truth underneath: if you're underposting, you're invisible. The algorithm rewards consistency. Audiences reward presence. We grew Reels output by more than 2,000% in a single year, and it was one of the single highest-impact decisions we made.

The key is building systems that let you post more without sacrificing quality: templated designs, a content calendar mapped to the events and programs we already knew were coming, and a clear content mix across education, promotion, community, and behind-the-scenes. We weren't reinventing the wheel every week.

Video changed everything

When we shifted to a video-first strategy, reach increased sharply, with video output on Facebook up 783% year over year. Short-form video gets preferential distribution on every major platform right now, and for a sports brand it's a natural fit. Action is inherently visual. A pickleball tournament clip, a tennis clinic highlight, a quick tip from a pro: these outperformed static graphics consistently.

Meet people where the algorithm already wants to meet them.

What the data actually said

Engagement rate per impression actually decreased as reach grew. That's normal and expected. When you reach new audiences, they engage at lower rates than your existing followers do. So I stopped treating engagement rate as the headline number. The metrics I focused on instead were net new follower growth and the correlation between social reach and web traffic, because social is a top-of-funnel channel, not a vanity scoreboard.

The unsexy truth

Most social growth doesn't come from a viral moment. It comes from showing up consistently, reading your data honestly, and iterating. We tested formats, analyzed what performed, doubled down on winners, and cut what wasn't working. Across the full network in 2023, that added up to 7,354 posts, 3,115 of them Reels, and a combined 11.7M cross-platform impressions. That's the whole strategy.
