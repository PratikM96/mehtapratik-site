Redesigning an App for 17M Users: What I Would Do Differently

DealNewsProduct design · iOS & Android7 min read

When I led the visual redesign of the DealNews iOS and Android apps, the user base was 17 million monthly users. That number is exciting. It also means every design decision has consequences at scale, and you can't afford to be cavalier. We shipped a redesign that meaningfully improved the product. Looking back, there are things I'd approach differently.

What we shipped

Third-party login with Google, Apple, and Facebook. A new filter bar. Updated iconography aligned to the website. Saved searches synced across platforms. Modal interactions for marketing messages and tips. Every change addressed a specific friction point, executed in collaboration with the dev team on handoff and implementation. The rollout drove an 18% lift in on-site conversions and moved app store ratings from roughly 3.8 to 4.6 on Google Play and 4.7 on the App Store.

I'd push for more user research upfront

The redesign was driven largely by internal pain points and stakeholder feedback. We knew the app was outdated. We knew login was creating friction. What we didn't have was robust data on how users were actually navigating the existing app before we changed it.

Heatmaps, session recordings, and exit surveys would have given us a clearer picture of where the real problems were. Some of what we assumed was broken turned out to be fine. Some things we didn't plan to touch probably should have been redesigned.

At scale, every assumption deserves scrutiny.

I'd define success metrics before launch, not after

We had a sense of what we wanted to improve: login conversion, session length, engagement with deals. But we didn't formalize those as targets before we shipped. That made it harder to evaluate the redesign rigorously in the weeks after launch. Always define your north-star metric before you start designing. It keeps the team aligned and gives you a clear way to know whether the work moved the needle.

I'd invest more in the transition experience

When you change an app that 17 million people use habitually, some of them will be disoriented. We updated iconography and navigation patterns, the right call for consistency, but we didn't invest enough in guiding users through what changed and why. A simple onboarding overlay highlighting the new features would have helped. It's a small thing that makes a real difference in how change is received.

The takeaway

The redesign accomplished what it set out to do: it modernized the product and closed the gap between the app and the web experience, with the conversion and rating numbers to show for it. But the process taught me that scale magnifies everything, including your blind spots. Research, metrics, and a transition plan aren't overhead. At 17 million users, they're the difference between a redesign you can defend and one you just hope worked.
